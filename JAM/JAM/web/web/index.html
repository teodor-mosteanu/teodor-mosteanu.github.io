About JAM

The world may have changed but the human need to share stories has not, in fact it has become more important than ever and JAM 2020 seeks to reflect this in its first ever virtual conference. The act of storytelling involves a process of choice making – we choose the details to include and exclude, we shape narratives depending on who we share our stories with and how. Originally a live event, this conference is interested in exploring methods and approaches to sharing stories in theatre, performance, film, television, and literary practice.  

It promises to be an interesting day, packed with varied research. There will be theatre workshops, installations, film essays, posters and discussions on: 

 

  • Evolution of the modern sitcom 
  • Visual Representations of Young Saudi Women in Britain 
  • Immersive Storytelling and Postphenomenological Performance
  • Objects in Abbas Kiarostami’s filmmaking 
  • Trauma in Eimear McBride's "A Girl is a Half formed Thing"
  • Home in the cinema 
  • Food storytelling 
  • Spatial exclusion of Black women in British theatre 
  • Male gaze and Kuwaiti cinema 
  • Lived experiences of Woodstock 
  • Set design in Renata Pinheiro's "Love, Plastic and Noise" 
  • Playable Performance and Interpersonal Storytelling

DEPARTMENT OF FILM, THEATRE AND TELEVISION carries out research under the University's Heritage & Creativity Research Theme.

Our research analyses the languages and conventions of film, theatre and performance, television, and new media. We explore their cultural impact and how they engage with identity and representation, inclusion and social justice, conflict, and migration.

Our postgraduate research students range from recent graduates just beginning their careers to established industry professionals.

We host an annual conference, Journeys Across Media, which is an opportunity for students to meet and learn about each other's work. This year’s conference is being held as an online event for the very first time in its history.

We frequently collaborate with other departments at Reading on research projects.

For example, we partnered with the Department of Politics and International Relations on Disappearing War: Cinema and the Politics of Erasure in the War on Terror.

We are based in Minghella Studios, which houses a collection of versatile performance spaces and facilities to support staff and postgraduate research practice.

We also host talks, workshops, residencies and public performances by eminent scholars and practitioners.

Within the Department, we have more than 9,000 audiovisual assets for film, theatre and television research.

Sarah Byrne ( Postgraduate Researcher in FTT )

Sarah Byrne is a PhD student in the Film, Theatre, and Television department at the University of Reading. Her interdisciplinary research is concerned with the hybrid form of livecasting. The primary areas of interest are remediation and the processes of medium hybridity and aesthetic hybridity that livecasting utilises. Sarah has previously presented at the Screen Studies Conference in Glasgow.

Abstract - Recreating the livecast experience without the live? The dual temporality of National Theatre At Home

Building on a larger thesis project on the structure and aesthetics of livecast theatre, this paper seeks to address the unique temporal duality of the context in which National Theatre at Home had to operate in lockdown. On the one hand the broadcasts drew from material from the previous ten years while at the same time the on-going distribution of those performances was prompted by the current pandemic and the various social and political events that happened within and around it. National Theatre at Home operates in a highly contextualised space and this paper will use a combination of scholarship on liveness and live experience and close analysis to argue that these innovations put new challenges on the question on medium specificity.

Magdalena Mosteanu

Magdalena Mosteanu is a theatre practitioner, PhD candidate and Sessional Lecturer at Film Theatre and Television Department, University of Reading, UK. Gained her BA and MA in Acting (Warsaw, 2014) as well as MA in Collaborative Theatre Making (London/Coventry 2017) run in association with Frantic Assembly. Currently investigating in her practise-as-research project frameworks and methodologies of interweaving cultures in theatre.

Abstract - Theatrical attempts to bridge cultures through food storytelling - Easy as Pie online installation.

In an answer to the question of dissemination of research stories, I wish to propose an online installation documenting a series of storytelling workshops which took place in July 2019 at the Museum of English Rural Life in Reading. Easy as Pie, in its online version, is placed at the intersection of research, practice and the digital space. This practice-led research reveals the correlations of migration and food storytelling, places the food stories as markers of intercultural mediation and signifiers of a common frame of reference within which people can communicate more freely. In an attempt to engage with the stories of ‘the others’ I invite the audience to reflect on the ways of listening that begin to unravel the relationship between the linguistic message and bodily understanding. Easy as Pie is a call for listening as a creative, embodied and bonding act of communication. As Martin Iddon (2010) puts it:

It is worth noting, too, the correspondence between the auricle, the external part of the ear and the left and right auricles: muscle pouches attached to the atria of the heart. The ear and the heart are, in a sense, both involved in what it might mean to ‘listen in’.


In a sense, Ease as Pie, invites to ‘listen in’ to the personal stories of the participants, whose narratives are entangled around their experiences of food, migration and bring a sense of home to the listener with truly open ears.

Amina Boukheloua

Amina Boukheloua, an international second-year PhD student at the University of Leeds, at the School of Sociology and Social Policy. My areas of focus are women identities, women and migration, the identity of women through photography, and the effect of religious and social guidelines on Muslim women.

Abstract - Young Saudi Women in Britain: Context Change and Identity

The use of visual material such as photographs represents a rich and insightful means to get into individuals identities by exploring their journeys and the way they evolve through time. The presented research focuses on women identities and the way a picture can capture their definitions of the components of their identities in different contexts. It undertakes the case of Saudi women on a transnational migratory movement and the way their identities interact with both the religious and social context differences. The argument used is that young Saudi women in Britain self-represent and negotiate their identities differently than they do when in their familiar environment. The transnational formation occurring in both spaces (Saudi Arabia and Britain) affects the way these women negotiate their identities in which the negotiation process does not merely apply to one space but rather composes of traits either acquired or developed in both the sending and receiving societies. The research uses the qualitative case study approach implementing auto photography and photo elicitation to conduct both individual and in-depth group interviews. The aim is to explore the unique experiences of Arab Muslim women in self-expressing and negotiating their identities when changing their social-religious contexts through transnational migration.

James Kenward

James Kenward is a Postgraduate Researcher and Sessional Lecturer in the Department of Film, Theatre and Television at the University of Reading, England. His research concerns expressions of and approaches to reality in film, particularly in regards to Iranian Cinema. He is currently writing his thesis examining the intersections of politics and realism in the films of Abbas Kiarostami and Jafar Panahi.

Abstract - Objects in the Films of Abbas Kiarostami

The act of looking has featured continuously throughout Abbas Kiarostami’s filmmaking. Often, this has involved characters within his films acting as surrogates, wandering through desolate landscapes in search of meaning. Occasionally, it has even involved Kiarostami himself. Yet what of the objects under their gaze? Almost all might seem incidental – an aeroplane flying above, a bone flowing down a stream, an aerosol can rolling down a street – yet their cumulative effect is to create films brimming with objects. Sometimes, as is the case in Five, these objects take over the entire film, removing all narrative concerns in favour of a narrative built from the incidental – built from the nothingness. Yet it is precisely in this sparseness that Jean-Luc Nancy finds the full mobilisation of Kiarostami’s filmic gaze. In and of themselves, each of these objects might not signify or represent anything. This, however, is exactly the point. Freed of relation or signification, the objects in these films are left to exist for themselves.

Presentations of a real world beyond the limits of the film. It is not that these objects tell stories, but that through them the viewer might make their own.

Nadine Deller

I am first year PhD candidate, based at the Royal Central School of Speech and Drama. My research project, ‘Playing in the Dark’, is a collaboration between the National Theatre and RCSSD, concerned with illuminating the position of black women playwrights in the Black Plays Archive.

Abstract - Rethinking Stories of Exclusion: Black Women and Spatiality

This provocation concerns the exclusion of black women's stories in British theatre, by using ‘space’ as a key point of interrogation. This presentation concerns recent black women's theatre (Chino Odimba, 2019, Janice Okoh, 2020) and ask how spatiality might prove a useful tool for reimagining the socio-political landscape outside of the theatrical space. Recent theatre scholarship has addressed the implications of the historic exclusion of black women's narratives, including close readings of text, interviews and historical analysis (Goodman, 1996, Osborne, 2003, Goddard, 2007, 2015). However, the question of how we might read particular black women’s histories through the lens of spatiality has not been addressed in-depth. This presentation will pose a possible solution to open up how we read and tell women’s histories in theatre. Drawing on Joanne Tompkins’s work on Foucault's heterotopias (2014, 2019) - how spaces on stage connect with social and political realities off stage will be discussed in relation to how black women’s identities are articulated.

Can we open up discussions of how ‘space' might enable black women’s stories to be read/told in ways that imagine an alternative to the current social order?

Amelia Chisholm

Amelia Chisholm is currently studying her MA in Film Theory at King’s College London, after which she hopes to continue to a PHD. Her research interests lie in the significance of the sitcom as a form of text, the genre’s history and current trends, and the influence of comedy.

Abstract - “A Literal Marriage between Cynicism and Optimism”: The Evolution of the Modern Sitcom, and Schitt’s Creek.

Michial Farmer ‘s essay on ‘metamodernist sensibilities’ within the modern sitcom correctly identifies a contemporary trend of the genre’s humour moving from that of superiority, towards a “post-sincere, post-ironic approach”. This ‘metamodernist’ sitcom demands a cynical attitude towards characters, but a simultaneous belief in their positive intentions. It is this positivity which is equally noted in David Ehrlich’s article discussing the rise of cinematic ‘nicecore’, in which the protagonist faces a cynical world with a kind and selfless attitude. Both texts discuss the incorporation of both cynicism and sincerity within modern media. This conference proposal aims to explore not solely the accuracy of these claims, with a focus on the sitcom Schitt’s Creek, but to understand how far “we are brought into… idealism as participants”, as Farmer claims. The trend is significant; it demonstrates both a rejection of the sitcom as escapism from social convention, and a forced alliance with the main cast. This creates the potential for audience growth, alongside the protagonists. I believe that this demonstrates both the birth of a new subgenre, and the formation of a new relationship between audience and media.

Bibliography: Farmer, Michial. “’Cloaked In, Like, Fifteen Layers of Irony’: The Metamodernist Sensibility of “Parks and Recreation”. Studies in Popular Culture. Vol. 37, No.2. 2015. 103-120. Ehrlich, David. “The New Wave of Nicecore: How the Dark Age of Donald Trump Is Inspiring Movies to Choose Kindness over Conflict”. IndieWire. June 15th 2018. Accessed January 21st 2020, https://www.indiewire.com/2018/06/nice-movies-in-age-of-trump-1201974749/ . Filmography Schitt’s Creek. Not a Real Company Productions. 2015-2020.

Abstract - A Woman’s Place: Home in the Cinema

An exploration of emotional architecture in cinema, focusing on the figure of a woman in a house. Sounds and images from films made between 1936 and 2013 are woven together into a cinematic triptych spread laterally upon three screens, enabling translations, comparisons, resonances and vibrations between films to emerge. Silvia Macedo

Silvia Macedo is a Brazilian architect, production designer, and film producer with master's in urban theory. Currently, she is finishing a PhD in Film studies under the guidance of Professors Lucia Nagib and John Gibbs. The focus of her research is the visual identity of Northeast Brazil (re)created from the mid-1990s within the revival of Brazilian Cinema on the light of production design work.

Abstract - Set design and cityscape, challenges in the art direction’s composition

This paper will consider the representation of urban life in the film Amor, Plastico e Barulho (Love, Plastic and Noise, Renata Pinheiro, 2013). It will address the socioeconomic and cultural structure of the city of Recife – Northeast Brazil and the use of urban space and real locations as an identity element through the production design analysis. The film revolves around the lives of residents of a favela who aspire to success in the popular music business. The focus of the narrative is the conflicts caused by the ephemerality of artistic success, by the disposable character of things and people in a big city. The urban transformation linked to the economic growth at the beginning of the 21st century is revealed in the film through representations of a city that is verticalized with massive and increasing construction.

The film works on two aesthetic principles: the first is a documentary style, presenting the physical and social space of Recife; the second is that of a constructed visual composition and a peculiar mise-en-scène. This visual foundation base structured by the production design is strengthened by a camera that circulates freely around the set, without the limitations of a scenographic space made in studios.

Daniel O’Brien

Daniel O’Brien is a lecturer in Film and Digital Media at the University of Essex. He has a PhD in Film and Television Studies from the University of Glasgow and an MA in Film and Screen Cultures from Goldsmiths, University of London. His areas of research span across Film Studies, Interactive Media Art and Computer Gaming.

Abstract - Immersive Storytelling and Postphenomenological Performance in Blast Theory

Immersive storytelling across theatre, film and television is increasingly becoming a co-evolutionary practice between the artist and audience. From Punchdrunk Theatre company to Blast Theory, audiences are becoming actively involved in the unfolding of narrative through digital devices that negotiate with a user’s physical presence. Embodiment, as Don Ihde argues, is crucial to a specific type of postphenomenological experiencing through a fusion of the body and a technological device. This paper adopts Ihde to consider the role of the postphenomenological performer by discussing the trajectory of immersive story worlds. It considers two distinct early interactive artworks. Blast Theory’s A Machine to See With (2010) and Karen (2015) are two unique fictional pervasive games that enable users, through the technology of their mobile phone, to become immersed within a story world. Each artwork experience approaches the immersion differently, the former taking over an intense hour uses the physicality of the user and technological GPS properties to offer a type of locative cinema. The latter enables a different type of pervasive immersion through interstitial storytelling over a two-week period, incorporating the user’s smartphone into a virtual narrative space that pertains to a type of virtual socializing. Each artwork is considered through Ihde’s philosophy of technology that changes story readers into postphenomenological performers.

John Whitney

John Whitney is a doctoral candidate and sessional lecturer in the Department of Film, Theatre and Television at the University of Reading. His practice based research analyses the relationship between play and performance, and the social/personal rewards that game experiences can provide performance. John teaches on modules on queer theory, race and participatory theatres.

Game Rewards and Playable Performance: Reflections on Uncertain Times

Over the last six months, I’ve found myself playing a lot more games. Not just solo games, but games with others – whether we are exploring a castle with me as the dungeon master or trying to terraform a different planet together. This way of socialising became common for many, as the Covid-19 lockdown forced our social interactions to be facilitated in new ways. This resonates with my research into the social relationships that emerge from playable performance environments and with the practice as research dimension of work, which had to shift in both time and space over the past few months. My research investigates the close relationship between game play and performance experience. I use this relationship to identify an emerging performance style within the current performance canon: playable performance. In this short paper, I will discuss the rewards that emerge out of playful environments and the potential that this can have for playable performance experiences. I will conclude the paper by briefly discussing the methods through which I look to apply these thoughts within my practice as research context and the shifts/changes that have occurred in my thoughts around practice over the recent lockdown period.

Simon Aeppli

I have made short film work that has been screened at festivals, galleries and artist events in Britain and abroad. Recently, I received an AHRC technē scholarship for part-time PhD study at the University of Brighton for a project called ‘Operation Bogeyman: The Folk Horror Landscape of 1970's Northern Ireland’.

The Folk Horror Landscape of 1970's Northern Ireland

This paper focuses on my ongoing research into a film project, which investigates the story of how a clandestine military organisation called the Information Policy Unit (IPU), coordinated fake satanic rituals throughout Northern Ireland during the early 70’s to create terror and confusion across the province. The IPU used fear and superstition as a kind of technology to tap into and confabulate the space between local supernatural lore and organised religion and is possibly one of the strangest tactics carried out by the British Military during the conflict.

Drawing on first-hand accounts from ex-intelligence officer, Colin Wallace and the original newspaper reports the paper explores the correlation between the staged imagery of the faked rituals and the iconic folk horror films being produced in the UK in the 70’s. The research presents the idea that Wallace was working in a similar way to screenwriters by creating a real-time horror film in the landscape. There are uncanny parallels between Wallace’s ‘witchcraft operations’ and the narratives being produced under the genre of folk horror. The research forces us to relook at the use of psychological warfare and manipulation of the media in Northern Ireland by the British Military establishment but also asks the question - what does it mean to be a horror film in a time of horror?
